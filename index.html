What is Day Trading?
Lesson 1 of 4
1
Day trading = buying and selling the same day
You buy something in the morning. The price goes up. You sell it in the afternoon and keep the profit. That's a day trade. Everything happens within one day — you never hold overnight.
2
You can profit when prices go UP or DOWN
Most people only know about buying something and hoping it goes up. But traders can also "short" — which means profiting when a price falls. We'll teach you both.
3
The goal: $100–$200 extra per week to start
Not millions. Not overnight. Consistency is the goal. A trader making $150 a week reliably is doing better than someone swinging for $10,000 and losing everything.
Real world analogy: Imagine you notice sneakers on sale for $80 that you know sell for $120 on eBay. You buy them, flip them the same day, and pocket $40. That's exactly what day trading is — just with stocks, currencies, or other assets instead of sneakers.

What is a Candlestick?
Lesson 2 of 4
1
Each candle = one period of time
On a 1-minute chart, each candle covers 1 minute. On a daily chart, each candle covers one whole day. The candle shows you everything that happened during that time period.

The Fat Part (Body)Shows where the price opened and where it closed. Big body = strong move.
The Thin Lines (Wicks)Show the highest and lowest prices reached. Long wick = big fight.
Green = BullishPrice closed HIGHER than it opened. Buyers were in control.
Red = BearishPrice closed LOWER than it opened. Sellers were in control.
Real world analogy: Think of each candle like a tug-of-war score. Green = buyers pulled harder and won. Red = sellers pulled harder and won. The size of the candle tells you HOW much they won by. Long wicks mean it was a close fight.

The most important rule in all of trading
Before you make a single trade, you need to understand this. This one concept will save your account more times than anything else.
The Stop Loss — Your Seatbelt
Lesson 3 of 4
1
A stop loss = your automatic exit if the trade goes wrong
Before you buy anything, you decide: "If this drops to [price], I get out automatically." That price is your stop loss. You set it and forget it. No panic. No emotion. It just happens.
2
Example: You buy Nvidia at $870. Stop loss at $850.
If Nvidia drops to $850, your position sells automatically. You lose $20 per share — and not a penny more. Without a stop loss, you might hold and watch it drop to $700.
3
The 1% rule — never risk more than 1% per trade
With $500, that's $5 maximum risk per trade. Sounds tiny — it's not. This rule is why professional traders survive for years while amateurs blow up their accounts in weeks.
Real world analogy: A stop loss is exactly like your car's seatbelt. You don't plan to crash. But if you do, the seatbelt stops the damage from being catastrophic. Always buckle up before you drive — set your stop loss before every trade.
⚠️ The #1 beginner mistake: Removing your stop loss because "it'll come back." Sometimes it does. Sometimes it goes from $870 to $500. The stop loss removes that decision from your emotional hands.

How to Make a Trade
Lesson 4 of 4
1
Read the situation first — don't rush
Every trade scenario shows you a chart and news. Read the news carefully. What's happening to this stock? Is it good news or bad news? Is the chart going up or down?
2
You have 3 choices every time
BUY / LONG — You think the price is going UP. You buy and profit if it rises.

SELL / SHORT — You think the price is going DOWN. You profit if it falls.

HOLD — No clear signal. The smartest move is sometimes doing nothing.
3
After every trade — read the explanation
Whether you're right or wrong, the app tells you exactly why — in plain English. Every wrong trade is a free lesson. This is the whole point of practicing with fake money.
